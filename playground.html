The Starring Players

Moblins

Moblins have transmogrified so much from their early bulldog-like appearance to the Gonk Anteater we got in Breath of the Wild that they’re one of the best indicators of the game’s art direction (alongside Stalfos, Octoroks, ChuChus, Poes, etc.). The Sacred Forest Meadow bouncers of Ocarina went down hilariously easy with a shot or three, despite looking like bulldog-men on a steady supply of ‘roids fresh from the Lakeside Scientist’s laboratory.

Most of the 2D Moblins just look like generic pig humanoids (Link’s Awakening and the Oracles bizarrely includes the canine Moblins as well as porcine “Boarblins” – come on, commit one way or the other!). Wind Waker gave them some tubbiness and an extremely amusing floppy lower lip. They could still fuck you up though, stealth-section or not. I still get taken aback when a punch from out of nowhere sends Link flying across the map.

After a brief glimpse of their canceled Twilight Princess appearance, Moblins returned in their ugliest form yet for Skyward Sword, where they serve as giant, enormously obese blockades. Nobody could love these guys.

The Breath of the Wild Moblins retain some of Wind Waker’s floppiness in their snout. They also look a damn sight better than they did in Skyward Sword, and they will not hesitate to hurl their vertically-challenged Bokoblin brethren in a fight.

Likelihood of Reappearance: All But Guaranteed

The New Kids

Yiga Clan

The “evil Sheikah” concept that birthed the Yiga seems like that of poor fanfiction, but the Yiga are actually fairly well-executed. They become painfully obvious to spot after a few bouts with mysterious roadside travelers, but stalking their hideout is one of the more memorable setpieces in Breath of the Wild, their banana addiction is goofy enough to make them endearing, and their combat style gets its best use not in Master Kohga but in Maz Koshia, who is probably the best boss in the entire game, period.

I also like that they are, in a way, the successors of the Garo tribe, both in combat and in plot functionality. The cultured-ninja enemy was too good a concept to discard.

Gibdos

Questionable distinctiveness from ReDeads aside, the Gibdos nevertheless seem like they’re on the way out. After making a strong debut in the NES original and getting an entire plot sequence dedicated to them in Majora’s Mask, they seem to have fallen off the radar. Their last appearance to date was in Twilight Princess (though I remember a time when the sword-wielding mummies in Arbiter’s Grounds were called ReDead Knights), and it’s a good change up from the rathe drab evil-mummy look of all their other incarnations.

Did you know that Gibdos actually behave slightly differently than ReDeads? It’s true! Gibdos won’t approach their fallen brethren, cutting out one of Link’s windows for attack (though the Sun’s Song still stuns them).

Wolfos

The humanoid Wolfos only really appeared in Ocarina and Majora – and even then, they were running around on all fours most of the time! Still, they were a decent foe for sword combat with their inexplicably metallic-sounding fur, a less powerful but more agile counterpart to Stalfos. The “Wolfos” of Twilight Princess and Spirit Tracks could have dropped the -fos, as they were basically just regular old demonic wolves.

Likelihood of Reappearance: Not On Two Legs

Miniblins

Phantoms/Reaplings/Silent Realm Guardians

Grouped loosely together because of their invulnerability and generally Terminator-esque behavior, these monsters filled primarily a story role in their respective games. If a Zelda story in the future requires a similar enemy (the Guardians of Breath of the Wild actually fill a similar niche, though are obviously not humanoid), we might see the return of big scary good-or-evil knights. But it probably won’t be these specific kinds.

Eyegores

Proof that there’s room for only one Cyclopean enemy in the Zelda series, the Eyegores never quite broke out of the enemy C-listers, despite a memorable appearance at Stone Tower Temple in Majora’s Mask. Their gameplay mechanic – staying still until approached, then retreating – was largely overtaken by Armos.

Likelihood of Reappearance: Not Until Hinox Disappear

Wait, Who?

The one-offs, weirdoes, and wannabes of the Zelda series. Maybe they never differentiated themselves from the competition, or maybe they were designed around a unique gameplay mechanic that never resurfaced. Regardless, these enemies are likely to make even a well-versed Zelda fan scratch their head.

Chilfos

Though they provided a handful of memorable moments during their sole appearance in Twilight Princess (the fight among the pews in Snowpeak Mansion is a highlight), the Chilfos were doomed from the start by being an enemy tailor-made for a one-of-a-kind dungeon item. A group of them could make your life miserable with their ranged spears and absurdly fast attack speed, but once you got your hands on the Ball and Chain, they went down like the spindly little icicles they were.

If the Ball and Chain ever returns to the series, these guys or some similarly fragile enemy should also reappear – there’s nothing like mowing down a line of them in a single throw.

Likelihood of Reappearance: Fat Chance for Thin Ice

Arm-Mimics

The Mask-Mimics were bound not to reappear for the same reasons that Chain Chomps, Goombas, and the like never made it past Link’s Awakening. The Arm-Mimics, though, were interesting enough design-wise that it’s sad they never showed up again. What are they – clay dolls? Golems? At least they got a spiritual successor in 3D space with the Anubii of Ocarina, a similarly forgotten enemy with untapped mechanic possibilities.

And what about those Temple of Time guardians in Twilight Princess?

Likelihood of Reappearance: No Way, No How

Yook

Mechanically, Yook are essentially bipedal Dodongos – and their sole appearance is in a game that already has Dodongos! Nevertheless, the primary appeal of Yook lies with them as NPCs. The Anouki are charming and distinctive but seem unlikely to break out of the DS ghetto. Meanwhile, Twilight Princess already featured two Yeti in one of its most memorable chapters. Is there a chance for Yook to return as a semi-Peaceable race in the future?

Likelihood of Reappearance: You Gotta Put Some Kind Of Race In Mount Hebra

Snappers

These Spirit Tracks one-offers set themselves apart from many others on this list by pulling Link closer in if he got within a certain range, and also by being weird, fat circus-owl-men. The sort of keeping-Link-captive gambit is only really used by Redeads and the like, and the whip is a great twist to that particular attack mechanic. It could even be upgraded too – imagine a miniboss who uses the dungeon’s hookshot to drag Link up close and attack.

Likelihood of Reappearance: Only After Makeover

Keatons

For a brief period of time, it seemed like the mythologically inspired Keaton was a rising star in the Zelda universe. After a cameo as a mask in Ocarina, a one-off appearance in Majora, and a regular enemy in Minish Cap, the Keatons were on a roll. Minish Cap, however, ended their brief career. There, they were basic field enemies which combined ground combat with the Rupee-stealing behavior of crows. The difficulty of implementing something similar in 3D is probably why they have never reappeared, though their “trickster” niche is one that could service them in a non-enemy role down the line.

Likelihood of Reappearance: Maybe As An NPC

Taros, Gimos, Freezors, Geldmen, Pikku, Pengators, Zazak

These would be the most obscure enemies in the mainline Zelda series, if it weren’t for…

Fokka, Daira, Geru, Parutamu, Fokkeru, Mago, Wosu, Doomknockers, Guma

…the existence of Zelda II. This was truly the heyday of random humanoid enemies, probably because the side-scrolling combat favored upright enemy design. Unfortunately, it never even began for many of these misfits, whose designs are generic and names far too Japanese-sounding (I’m sorry) to leave much of an impression.

There’s a few interesting lore questions raised here. Are the Fokka and Fokkeru the defected descendants of Rito? Who gave the Parutamu their spiffy sci-fi visors?
